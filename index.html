03 — Reference

Notation glossary

Symbols that appear in almost every proof-based course.

SymbolTermMeaning
∀For all"For all x in S, P(x)" — the statement must hold for every element, no exceptions.
∃There exists"There exists x in S such that P(x)" — you only need one example (a witness).
¬ / "not"Negation¬P is the opposite of P. Negating "for all" gives "there exists...not". Negating "there exists" gives "for all...not".
→ImpliesP → Q: "if P then Q." The hypothesis P is what you're given; the conclusion Q is what you must show.
↔If and only ifP ↔ Q means P → Q and Q → P. Both directions must be proved separately.
⊥ContradictionTwo statements that cannot both be true — the target in proof by contradiction.
□ / QEDEnd of proofSignals the proof is complete. QED = quod erat demonstrandum ("what was to be shown").
WLOGWithout loss of generalityThe argument is symmetric, so proving one case covers all. Must be justified explicitly.
ℕ, ℤ, ℚ, ℝNumber setsNatural numbers (0,1,2,...), integers (...−1,0,1,...), rationals, reals. Know which set your variable lives in.
